1. A Comparison (Indirect and Not Fair) of Similar Variables Between the Bus and the Train

Since we don't have all the data we need yet, we will at least work with what we have and see what happens. Note that this is to see a general trend we might expect, or just performing statistical analysis for the sake of doing so, because as we are comparing northbound and southbound runs on different modes, important variables are not being controlled, and therefore, our comparison here generally should not be taken too seriously. As we do our statistical analysis, we will need to find the mean and root mean squared error (RMSE). The mean is simply the average, the sum of all data point values divided by the number of data points. It generally serves to show a good approximation of all the data points as a single value. The RMSE is a bit more complicated. To find the RMSE, we start by finding the difference between each data point and the mean. We square each difference, and then sum them together for all data points. We then divide the sum by the number of datapoints minus 1, then take the square root of the number. This is our RMSE, and is a measure of how spread out our data points are from the aforementioned mean.
